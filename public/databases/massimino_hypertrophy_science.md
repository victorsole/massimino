## What Actually Drives Muscle Growth

### Mechanical Tension Is King

Mechanical tension — the force generated by muscle fibers during contraction — is the most potent non-pharmacological stimulus for increasing skeletal muscle mass. This is not debatable; it is the most well-supported mechanism in the literature.

**How it works, simplified:**

1. You lift weights (resistance exercise training, or RET).
2. Mechanosensors in your muscle fibers detect the force.
3. These sensors trigger intracellular signaling cascades (primarily the mTORC1 pathway).
4. Muscle protein synthesis (MPS) increases, exceeding muscle protein breakdown (MPB).
5. Over time, this net positive protein balance leads to muscle fiber growth (hypertrophy).

**Key insight for trainers:** Hypertrophy can be achieved across a wide range of loads, from light to heavy, as long as mechanical tension is sufficient. This means program design can be flexible and personalized.

### Mechanotransduction: How Muscles "Sense" Force

Mechanotransduction is the process by which physical force is converted into molecular signals inside muscle cells. Key molecular players include:

- **Integrins (α7β1-integrin):** Protein complexes that link the outside of the cell to the inside, acting as mechanical sensors.
- **Focal Adhesion Kinase (FAK):** An enzyme activated during mechanical overload that helps initiate growth signals.
- **Titin:** A large elastic protein that responds to sarcomere stretching during eccentric (lowering) contractions.
- **mTORC1 pathway:** The central hub for protein synthesis signaling, activated by mechanical loading.

**Key insight for trainers:** The type of muscle action matters. Dynamic contractions (standard weight training) primarily increase muscle cross-sectional area, while stretch-induced loading may promote sarcomere addition in series (longitudinal growth).

## Myths Debunked: What Does NOT Drive Muscle Growth

### MYTH 1: Acute Hormonal Spikes Build Muscle

**The claim:** Post-exercise spikes in testosterone, growth hormone (GH), and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) drive hypertrophy.

**The evidence against it:**

- Training one arm under high-hormone conditions vs. low-hormone conditions produces identical hypertrophy (12% vs. 10%, no significant difference).
- In a study of 56 participants, post-exercise rises in GH, free testosterone, and IGF-1 showed zero correlation with gains in lean body mass or strength.
- Females have 10–20x lower total testosterone and up to 200x lower free testosterone than males, yet multiple meta-analyses show similar relative gains in muscle mass following resistance training.
- Postmenopausal women gain muscle comparably to age-matched men.
- No sex-based differences exist in muscle protein synthesis rates over 24 hours post-exercise.

**Bottom line for Massimino users:** Do not design your workouts around "boosting testosterone" or "maximizing growth hormone." These acute hormonal changes are byproducts of exercise, not drivers of growth.

> **Important distinction:** This refers to natural endogenous hormone fluctuations. Exogenous anabolic steroids (supraphysiological doses) do dramatically increase muscle mass — one study showed 6.1 kg of fat-free mass gain from 600 mg/week of testosterone over just 10 weeks.

### MYTH 2: Metabolic Stress and Lactate Build Muscle

**The claim:** The "burn" from lactate, hydrogen ions, and inorganic phosphate accumulation during high-rep sets directly stimulates hypertrophy.

**The evidence against it:**

- When researchers infused sodium lactate during resistance exercise (raising blood lactate ~130% higher), it did not alter mTOR signaling, protein synthesis rates, or intramuscular pH.
- In mice, 10 days of mechanical overload produced 43.7% hypertrophy with zero increase in protein lactylation.
- Adding blood flow restriction (BFR) to 80% 1RM training increased metabolic stress markers but produced no additional hypertrophy vs. the same training without BFR.
- Exercise modalities producing equal or greater metabolite concentrations (sprinting, HIIT) result in significantly less hypertrophy than resistance training.

**What metabolites actually do:** They may contribute to fatigue-induced recruitment of higher-threshold motor units, which then experience mechanical tension and grow. The metabolites themselves are not anabolic — they just help recruit more muscle fibers indirectly.

**Bottom line for Massimino users:** High-rep "pump" sets are not inherently superior for growth. Both high-load/low-rep and moderate-load/moderate-rep training produce similar hypertrophy when total volume is equated.

### MYTH 3: "The Pump" Builds Muscle

**The claim:** Cell swelling (intracellular fluid accumulation) during exercise directly stimulates protein accretion and hypertrophy.

**The evidence against it:**

- Most mechanistic work on cell swelling and protein synthesis was conducted in liver cells (hepatocytes), not muscle cells.
- The two human studies that found correlations between acute swelling and hypertrophy did not compare different protocols, had methodological limitations, and at best explained ~25% of variance using single-variable correlations.
- A study comparing participants with significantly different post-exercise blood flow (37–60% greater in one group) found no differences in hypertrophy when both groups trained to failure at the same relative load.
- Blood flow restriction applied without exercise does not stimulate muscle protein synthesis.

**Bottom line for Massimino users:** Chasing "the pump" is fine if you enjoy it and it keeps you motivated, but it should never be the primary goal of your program. It has no demonstrated impact on long-term muscle growth.

### MYTH 4: Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy Is a Major Growth Mechanism

**The claim:** Muscles can grow by expanding the non-contractile sarcoplasm (fluid, glycogen, enzymes) rather than adding contractile proteins (myofibrils), and this is a meaningful training adaptation.

**The evidence against it:**

- The key study supporting sarcoplasmic hypertrophy (Haun et al., 2019) had a non-significant main ANOVA for fiber cross-sectional area (p = 0.152), non-significant decreases in actin and myosin (p = 0.052 and 0.055), and results potentially driven by a single statistical outlier.
- Multiple tracer studies show that resistance training robustly increases myofibrillar (contractile) protein synthesis with minimal effect on sarcoplasmic protein synthesis.
- A 2024 study using a novel imaging method confirmed that muscle fiber growth is primarily driven by myofibrillogenesis (addition of contractile proteins), not sarcoplasmic expansion.
- Studies in both untrained and trained individuals consistently show proportional increases in myofibrillar content alongside fiber growth.

**Bottom line for Massimino users:** Your muscle growth is predominantly real, functional, contractile tissue. The idea that bodybuilders build "non-functional" muscle is largely unsupported.

## How Much Muscle Can You Actually Gain?

### Short-Term Expectations (4–24 Weeks)

Based on a meta-analysis of 111 studies (951 male participants):

| Response Level | Fat-Free Mass (FFM) Gain |
|---|---|
| Average responder | ~1.5 kg |
| Low responder (bottom 10%) | ~0.4 kg |
| High responder (top 10%) | ~3.0 kg |
| High responder (top 2.5%) | >3.8 kg |
| Anabolic steroid user (600 mg/wk testosterone) | ~6.1 kg in 10 weeks |

### Long-Term Expectations (Career)

- Natural male bodybuilders typically reach an FFMI of 22.7–25.0.
- This translates to roughly 12.5–20.2 kg of total FFM gain, or approximately 2.5–4.0 kg per year over a 5-year training period.
- The most rapid hypertrophy occurs in the first 3–6 months; gains diminish substantially after that.
- Highly competitive bodybuilders showed no significant muscle fiber size changes over 24 weeks of training, demonstrating how hard it is to add muscle at advanced stages.

### Female Expectations

- Females experience similar relative gains to males.
- Absolute gains are approximately 70% of male values due to lower baseline FFM.
- A 1.5 kg gain in males equates to roughly 1.0 kg in females over the same period.
- A lifetime gain of 10 kg in males would be approximately 7 kg in females.

### Key Context

- Fat-free mass (FFM) includes bone, water, skin, organs — not just muscle. However, the primary tissue driving changes in FFMI over time is skeletal muscle.
- These values should set realistic expectations, not discourage anyone. The health benefits of resistance training extend far beyond muscle size.

## Evidence-Based Training Principles

Based on the findings of this review, effective resistance training for hypertrophy should prioritize:

1. **Progressive overload:** Gradually increase load, reps, or volume over time.
2. **Sufficient training volume:** There is a dose–response relationship between volume and hypertrophy, though highly variable between individuals.
3. **Proximity to failure:** Aim for approximately 2 repetitions in reserve (RIR) per set.
4. **Wide loading range:** Hypertrophy occurs across loads from ~30% to ~80%+ of 1RM when sets are taken near failure.
5. **Consistency and adherence:** The best program is one you can stick with long-term.
6. **Adequate recovery:** Sleep, nutrition, and rest days matter.
7. **Long-term perspective:** Think in months and years, not weeks.

### What NOT to Prioritize

- Workouts designed to spike hormones
- Chasing metabolic stress or "the burn" as a primary goal
- Maximizing "the pump" at the expense of mechanical tension
- Short rest periods solely to increase metabolic demand
